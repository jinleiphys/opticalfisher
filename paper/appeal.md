# Request for Reconsideration of Manuscript LM20073

*"Intrinsic information limit in nuclear optical potential extraction", J. Lei (sole author). Addressed to the Editors of Physical Review Letters.*

I ask the Editors to reconsider the decision on LM20073; a detailed point-by-point response accompanies this letter. The second-round decision was reached while Referee A's single technical question was still awaiting an answer; the resubmitted manuscript answers it quantitatively, without changing the underlying logarithmic-sensitivity analysis, and makes the scope amendments Referee B requested. The grounds for reconsideration are summarized below.

On innovation, Referee A is correct that "the main degeneracies found by the author are not necessarily new," and I do not claim otherwise. The same sentence states that "the work effectively packages them into a compact quantitative metric $D_\mathrm{eff}$." The innovation is what this quantitative formulation makes possible: a 168-configuration effective-dimensionality map, an exact statement about uniform precision rescaling within a fixed error model, and a decomposition of which additional data open independent parameter directions. The contribution is therefore not the rediscovery of the Igo ambiguity, but the quantitative answers and measurement hierarchy that the longstanding qualitative picture did not provide. King *et al.* [Phys. Rev. Lett. 122, 232502 (2019)] provide the empirical starting point: their Bayesian-frequentist comparison exposed strongly non-Gaussian optical-potential posteriors and the depth-radius correlation, but did not count the effectively constrained combinations, determine what uniform precision improvement changes within a fixed error model, or map the ambiguity across systems. The present work supplies those quantitative answers and converts the empirical observation into a measurement hierarchy.

On impact, the physics result is not the scale invariance of the participation ratio by itself; it is the observed stability of a strongly anisotropic, surface-dominated sensitivity geometry across 168 configurations, and the unequal ways in which the tested data classes broaden it. Within the local Fisher description and a fixed error model, uniform precision improvement reduces absolute uncertainties but rescales every eigenvalue together, leaving $D_\mathrm{eff}$ and the axis ratios unchanged: it tightens the same combinations without opening new independent directions, so changing the geometry requires data or model relations with different sensitivities. The broader relevance does not rest on treating KD02 as universal. The calculation identifies the physical mechanism, surface-dominated elastic scattering making the logarithmic depth and radius sensitivities nearly collinear, and the 168-configuration scan tests the stability of that geometry; the revised Letter presents KD02 as the quantified benchmark and states explicitly that verification with other global parameterizations is a next step.

Quantitatively, a single-energy elastic angular distribution determines $D_\mathrm{eff} = 1.7 \pm 0.4$ of 13 local parameters; multi-energy combination broadens the constrained subspace to about 2.3 to 2.5 on average and remains the largest broadening under either back-projection convention, and the full 48-coefficient KD02 analysis gives 2.9. The manuscript ranks the tested options by how effectively they broaden the constrained subspace, not by total information or posterior-volume reduction: at representative precision ratios, the 168-configuration observable census finds a smaller, neutron-dominated gain from $A_y$ and a negligible median gain from $\sigma_R$. This is bounded guidance for a documented community need: the revised Introduction cites Hebborn *et al.* [J. Phys. G 50, 060501 (2023)] for the rare-isotope-beam priority of quantifying and reducing the reaction-model uncertainties associated with optical potentials, and the hierarchy indicates which measurements separate parameter directions before those uncertainties are propagated to transfer, breakup, or charge-exchange observables.

Referee A, after the substantial first-round extension, describes the revised work as "comprehensive and quite convincing" and states that it "could be broadly impactful across a large subfield of the nuclear physics reaction community." The single technical question in the second report concerns forward- and backward-angle data under the relative-error model. The Referee is correct that the cross-section magnitude cancels from the Fisher matrix; the analysis already used logarithmic sensitivities, so this wording correction changes no numerical result. The incorrect explanatory sentence has been replaced by a direct collinearity test: for $n+{}^{40}$Ca at 50 MeV, 96% of the squared norm of the backward-angle $a_v$ sensitivity lies in the $V$-$r_v$ plane, the orthogonal remainder carries only $2\times10^{-3}$ of the leading Fisher eigenvalue, and backward-only, forward-only, and full-coverage analyses give $D_\mathrm{eff}=1.20$, 1.19, and 1.22. This resolves the one point the Referee asked to be addressed.

Referee B's objections concern interpretation and scope, and the revised manuscript addresses them in three ways. It restricts "sloppy" to the local Fisher-spectrum hierarchy in logarithmic KD02 coordinates, and no longer uses the local condition number to imply a literal global parameter excursion. It separates the information supplied by a single-energy elastic distribution from the constraints supplied by other observables and by folding, dispersive, and global relations, adding the nonlocal dispersive analysis of $^{40}$Ca as a direct nucleon-nucleus example. It also replaces the claim that the bottleneck is "universal" with "stable across the configurations sampled, with only weak dependence on," and removes the priority claim "first quantitative characterization." The accompanying response identifies the exact edits. I recognize that the Referee's recommendation for a more specialized journal remains, alongside the assessment that the work is "a valuable application of interesting statistical techniques." On breadth of impact the two reports point in different directions. Referee A sees potential for broad impact across a large subfield of the reaction community, whereas Referee B deems a specialized journal more appropriate.

The central conclusion is unchanged, but the numerical record was corrected. A final verification pass recomputed every number directly from the analysis scripts, found and fixed a high-energy partial-wave convergence deficiency, and certified all 168 configurations against $l_\mathrm{max}=90$ and 120, with no configuration's $D_\mathrm{eff}$ shifting by more than 0.012. The corrected values are those quoted above, and the accompanying response itemizes every numerical change.

In accordance with the APS policy on the appropriate use of AI tools, the revised manuscript now carries an explicit disclosure in the Acknowledgments: large-language-model assistants (Claude, Anthropic, Opus 4 series; Codex, OpenAI, GPT-5 series) were used to assist with developing and debugging the analysis code, to provide separate numerical cross-checks, and to revise the manuscript text. The verification pass described above was carried out within that workflow; I directed each use, checked numerical outputs against the archived analysis scripts and data, reviewed all text revisions, and take full responsibility for all content.

In light of Referee A's assessment of both the technical quality of this work and its potential for broad impact, I respectfully ask that the revised manuscript be reconsidered for publication in Physical Review Letters. The Letter does not claim that the Igo ambiguity itself is new. It contributes what the longstanding qualitative picture did not provide: the quantitative dimensionality map, the fixed-error-model precision statement, and the measurement hierarchy, with every conclusion bounded to KD02 and the stated error models.
