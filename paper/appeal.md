# Appeal on Manuscript LM20073

*"Structural information limit in nuclear optical potential extraction", J. Lei (sole author). Addressed to the Editors of Physical Review Letters.*

I ask the Editors to reconsider the decision on LM20073 through the formal appeal procedure. A detailed point-by-point response accompanies this letter; here I summarize the grounds for reconsideration.

Referee A, after the substantial first-round extension, describes the work as "comprehensive and quite convincing" and states that it "could be broadly impactful across a large subfield of the nuclear physics reaction community." The single technical question in the second report concerns forward- and backward-angle data under the relative-error model. The Referee is correct that the cross-section magnitude cancels from the Fisher matrix. The original analysis already used logarithmic sensitivities, so the numerical results do not change. I have replaced the incorrect explanation with a direct collinearity test: for $n+{}^{40}$Ca at 50 MeV, 96% of the squared norm of the backward-angle $a_v$ sensitivity lies in the $V$-$r_v$ plane, the orthogonal remainder carries only $2\times10^{-3}$ of the leading Fisher eigenvalue, and backward-only, forward-only, and full-coverage analyses give $D_\mathrm{eff}=1.20$, 1.19, and 1.22, respectively.

Referee B raises two interpretive objections. First, the revised manuscript now states precisely what is meant by "sloppy": in logarithmic KD02 coordinates and for the stated error models, the Fisher spectrum has the standard local hierarchy of a few stiff directions and many weakly constrained directions. I no longer use the local condition number to imply a literal global parameter excursion; establishing that range would require a profile-likelihood or model-manifold calculation. Second, I agree that radii, diffusenesses, and depths acquire constraints from established physics. The relevant distinction is the source of those constraints. A single-energy elastic angular distribution determines about 1.7 effective parameter combinations; additional observables, multi-energy systematics, folding structure, and dispersive relations constrain further combinations. The revised Discussion now states this complementarity directly. It also adds a nucleon-nucleus example from the nonlocal dispersive analysis of $^{40}$Ca, where elastic-equivalent local and nonlocal potentials yield different angular-momentum absorption, particle number, and charge density.

The manuscript's central quantitative result is unchanged. Across 168 configurations, single-energy elastic scattering gives $D_\mathrm{eff}=1.7\pm0.5$ out of 13 local parameters. Multi-energy systematics broaden the constrained subspace to about 2.4 dimensions on average, while the full 48-coefficient KD02 analysis gives $D_\mathrm{eff}=3.0$. This is a hierarchy for opening independent parameter directions, not a ranking of total Fisher information, which can increase even when the participation ratio decreases.

The result matters beyond a parameter-fit exercise. Optical potentials are inputs to transfer, breakup, and charge-exchange calculations at rare-isotope facilities. Before a discrepancy is assigned to reaction mechanism or structure, unconstrained optical-potential directions must either be fixed by additional physics or propagated to the observable. The manuscript gives a quantitative measure of that bottleneck and identifies which data classes broaden the constrained subspace.

For these reasons, and in light of Referee A's assessment of both technical quality and broad impact, I respectfully ask that the revised manuscript be reconsidered for publication in Physical Review Letters.
