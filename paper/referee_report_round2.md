Second Report of Referee A -- LM20073/Lei
----------------------------------------------------------------------

I'd like to thank the author for thoroughly addressing all of my comments from the previous report. The resulting work is now comprehensive and quite convincing (except for one point I raise below). As I noted in the first report, the main degeneracies found by the author are not necessarily new, but the work effectively packages them into a compact quantitative metric D_eff. And given the wide use of optical potentials by both theorists and experimentalists, I believe this work could be broadly impactful across a large subfield of the nuclear physics reaction community.

However, I ask the author to clarify a new comment that was made in the Reply and also on page 10 that I find confusing: "this independence contributes little to Deff because the backward-angle cross sections are orders of magnitude smaller, so the absolute Fisher information from these angles is negligible." Isn't the author assuming a relative error model, e.g., text around Eqs. (1) & (2)? Then the absolute magnitude of the cross section should scale out of the Fisher Information Matrix entries, right? A backward-angle point that is tiny in absolute cross section but highly shape-sensitive can matter just as much as a forward-angle point if measured to the same relative precision. This statement by the author makes me worried that the forward- and backward-angle data are not being treated on equal footing, even though in a relative error model they should be. This point needs to be addressed.

Report of Referee B -- LM20073/Lei
----------------------------------------------------------------------

This paper is concerned with a very old problem, namely the search for accurate and unique parameters in a potential model description of nuclei. In particular in the ’50s a complex potential (optical model) was introduced to describe elastic scattering and reaction cross sections.

First of all we should remind that ”a nuclear potential” is already a model and a complex potential to describe scattering, although phenomenologically very successful is very difficult to build microscopically starting from nucleon-nucleon interactions and ab-initio methods. At present there are successful potentials of this type but they are numerical and very hard to compare to phenomenological potentials of the Woods-Saxon geometry. Also there are optical potentials fitting nucleon-nucleus data but also others fitting a large variety of nucleus-nucleus combinations.

In this complicated physical contest the present author uses a very sophisticated statistical analysis to try to show that the optical potential belongs to the category of “ sloppy” models, according to Ref.[19]. However already the abstract of such reference states ”We explain why such systems so often are sloppy: the system behavior depends only on a few “stiff” combinations of the parameters and is unchanged as other “sloppy” parameter combinations vary by orders of magnitude.”

In the case of the optical model, parameters that cannot be uniquely fixed never change by orders of magnitude. Actually by trying to understand the physical meaning of the potential model parameters, beyond the technicalities of statistical models and/or quantification of uncertainties, many concepts have been clarified. Radii are now constraint by direct measurements, diffuseness has been shown to be linked to the separation energies of valence nucleons, and as a consequence depths can be constrained by systematical analysis of scattering in a large energy range.

This paper studies only the case of the Koning-Delaroche potential for nucleon-nucleus scattering and I do not agree with the “extreme” conclusions it reaches. However it is a valuable application of interesting statistical techniques. As such I deem it more appropriate to be published in a more specialized journal, provided the physical conclusions are amended along the lines I suggested above.
